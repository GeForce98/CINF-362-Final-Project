Table of Modules

Each of the modules on the website offer information about a range of topics that can help you make informed decisions and that you stay in compliance with the law, particularly as it relates to requirements that certain types of lobbying activities will require you and/or your organization to report such activities to New York State authorities on a regular basis.

Clicking on a Module will bring you to the corresponding Module section.

Module #1: What is Lobbying?

This module is designed to provide you with general information on lobbying, and to help you gain an understanding of lobbying concepts and terminology that appears throughout the tool.

Module #2: Am I a Lobbyist?

This module will help you to understand what type of activities constitute lobbying under New York State law, and therefore whether you will be required to comply with New York State reporting requirements.

Module #3: Do I Need to Report My Activities?

The information in this module will help you determine whether your organization meets certain thresholds related to lobbying that would require you to report your activities to New York State under state law.

Module #4: Direct Lobbying Via Social Media.

Direct lobbying can take place through the traditional activities outlined in Module #2, but they can also take place via more modern “social media” platforms. The information in this module is meant to help determine if your organization engages in direct lobbying via social media.

Module #5: Grassroots Lobbying Via Social Media.

Like direct lobbying, grassroots lobbying can take place through the traditional activities outlined in Module #2, and also through social media platforms such as Facebook and Twitter. The information in this module is meant to help determine if your organization engages in grassroots lobbying through these social media platforms.

Module #6: Social Media Attribution Test.

The information in this module is meant to help you determine if the social media activity of employees is attributable to your organization, which would impose a requirement to report the activities under New York State law.

Module #7: Lobbying Days.

This module provides information on lobbying days, an important tool that may be used by leaders of your organization to advocate their cause to politicians, and whether these activities must be reported under New York State law.

Module #8: Restrictions on Events and Prohibitions on Gifts.

In this final module, information is provided to help your organization determine whether certain events or certain planned gifts will require reporting to New York State under state lobbying laws.

Module 3 Section 1/9

Do I need to Report My Activities?

The information in this module is provided to help you determine if you need to report your organization’s activities in order to comply with New York State’s lobbying reporting requirements. First, this tool will direct you to review the activities that constitute lobbying. Reviewing these activities will be helpful if you need a reminder on which activities your organization conducts that you have determined constitute lobbying. Next, this tool explains the $5,000 expenditure threshold set by New York state. This threshold is important, because if you determine that lobbying expenses incurred by your organization exceed $5,000, you will be required to report them. Next, this module will explain and define the different types of expenses that you must account for when determining if your organization meets the $5,000 threshold. These expenses consist of reportable compensation, reportable expenses, funds spent on social media, and lobbying days. Each component is explained in its own step of the module. It is important to understand each of these concepts so you can determine what conduct will apply towards the threshold, and conclude if you meet the reporting requirement threshold.

Module 3 Section 3/9

Lobbying Expense

If your organization spends $5,000 or more on lobbying activities, you are required to disclose these activities to New York State. The following questions explain the various types of expenses that count towards this $5,000 threshold. To determine if you meet the $5,000 threshold, add up any expenses related to the activities you have deemed relevant.

Module 3 Section 4/9

Reportable Compensation

Reportable compensation counts towards the NYS threshold. Does your organization incur, expend, receive, or does your organization reasonably anticipate incurring, expending, or receiving reportable compensation [link to glossary] for lobbying activities on a state and/or local level per year? If you conclude that you do have reportable compensation, as defined by NYS law, this compensation will likely count towards the $5,000 threshold for lobbying activities referenced previously in this module.

Reportable Compensation: Money paid to an external lobbyist, or internal spending in exchange for lobbying or other services that furthers lobbying activity.

Examples: Spending qualifying as reportable compensation includes salaries of employees who lobby, fees, gifts, payments, benefits (but not fringe benefits), year-end bonuses, loans, advances, or any other thing of value paid, owed, given, or promised to the lobbyist by the client or employer.

Module 3 Section 5/9

Reportable Expenses

Reportable expenses count towards the NYS threshold. Does your organization incur, expend, receive, or will your organization reasonably anticipate incurring, expending, or receiving reportable expenses [link to glossary] for lobbying activities on a state or local level per year? If you conclude that you do have reportable expenses, as defined by NYS law, these expenses will likely count toward the $5,000 threshold for lobbying activities referenced previously in this module.

Reportable Expenses: Costs of lobbying activities incurred by or reimbursed in connection with a lobbying activity.

Examples: Spending qualifying as a reportable expense includes salaries paid to staff who do not lobby (such as professional and clerical staff), and spending for activities such as telephone expenses, electronic advocacy, food and beverages, tickets, entertainment, parties, receptions or events, advocacy rallies, consultant services, and courier services, when these activities are part of a lobbying effort.

Funds Spent on Social Media

Funds spent on social media lobbying [link to glossary] use will count towards the $5,000 threshold. Does your organization use social media to lobby? If you conclude that you do have expenses related to social media lobbying (direct or grassroots), as defined by NYS law, these expenses will likely count toward the $5,000 threshold for lobbying activities referenced previously in this module.

Reportable Expenses: for lobbying efforts taking place through social media can include consulting services, staff time allocated to planning and posting, and search engine optimization and sponsoring. To assess funds spent on social media usage, you should use a reasonable methodology to calculate expenses in good faith.

Module 3 Section 8/9

Exempt Activities

The following activities are considered statutorily exempt from reporting under New York State law

1. Non-Lobbying Legal Services

2. News Gathering and Publication

3. Contacts with the Media

4. Local Lobbying by IRC Section 6033 Religious Organizations.

5. Licenses and Permitting

6. Participation at Certain Public Proceedings

7. Adjudicatory Proceeding

8. Responses to Requests for Information

Does your organization take part in any of these statutorily exempt activities? If you conclude that you do, then you will not be required to include expenses related to these activities toward the $5,000 threshold for lobbying activities referenced previously in this module.

Module 3 Section 9/9

Total Expenses

Please add all reportable expenses [link to glossary] and reportable compensation [link to glossary], keeping in mind that social media use and lobby day expenses may be included in these sums. Be sure to not include any funds that are directed towards the statutorily exempt activities.

If you conclude that your total expenses related to lobbying activities exceed $5,000 you will likely need to report these under New York State law.

If you conclude that your total expenses related to lobbying activities do not exceed $5,000, you may not be required to report these expenses. However, please note that in certain circumstances you will be required to report expenses related to lobbying activities even if you do not exceed the $5,000 threshold. Make sure to seek legal counsel for questions or assistance in complying with the law.
